Characterizing Digital Divides Across the U.S.

Yijing Chen, Irena Chen, Megan Zhou

A digital divide is an uneven distribution in the access to or usage of Information and Communication Technologies (ICT), such as computers, smartphones, and Internet. Often, these digital divides reflect broader divides between socioeconomic, age, and racial/ethnic groups, and deepen existing inequities. In the US, inequalities in computer and internet access are driven by the high cost of broadband and by geography.

As the world increasingly moves online, digital divides become economic divides. COVID19 lockdowns across the US have highlighted how inequalities in broadband access translate into inequalities in educational advancement, employment opportunities, and even healthcare access.

So we wanted to investigate: what do these digital divides along socioeconomic and geographic lines actually look like in the US? How have they changed in recent years? And how might we close these divides?

1. Digital Divides across Demographic Groups


Across all years of data, those in the lowest income groups, older adults, and communities of color tend to have less access to Internet at home.
The expense of internet is the first or second most oft-cited reason for lack of access.

2. Recent geographic and regional trends


Southern states tend to report computer usage that falls below the national average,
while western states tend to report computer usage above the national average.

[Mouse over bars to see more details.]

Even though we see clear regional divides, most states that have less computer access compared to the national average do appear to have improved their gap over time. We can see this in North Carolina and South Carolina.

3. Municipal broadband: a growing solution?


Private internet providers have monopolies over their respective regions in the US, allowing them to drive up prices. Municipal broadband, or the provision of broadband by a public provider, has been proposed as one way to help areas with scarcer internet access bridge the divide.

The number of US residents with access to municipal broadband has steadily increased over the past two decades, but this solution still only reaches less than 1% of the entire US population.
